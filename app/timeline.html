What We've Learned from a Decade of Failures

The takeaways from tracking the tech debacles of the last ten years

Software and hardware are built by people, so no technology is immune to human error. When we embrace greater technological capability and complexity, we also increase our vulnerability when those systems fail. Ten years ago, IEEE Spectrum published the"Why Software Fails," an article that examined the underlying causes of notable project failures. A couple years later, we started the Risk Factor blog, with the goal of tracking technology failures both large and small.

We never ran out of things to write about. In fact, it's all too easy to get desensitized by constant failure—when multi-million-dollar debacles become commonplace, they lose their ability generate shock or outrage. Indeed, regular Risk Factor readers might wonder if complex software integration projects ever succeed. It's impossible to say whether IT failures are more frequent now than in the past (after all, the digitization of society has increased over the past decade), but it does seem that the aggregate consequences are worse.

To commemorate ten more years of failure, we decided to take a step back and look at the big picture, by compiling a rogue's-gallery of notable failures from the past decade (choosing approximately two failures per month). Keep in mind that the failures listed here are just the tip of the iceburg: they represent just a small fraction of the stories we've covered in the Risk Factor, which itself is far from comprehensive.
